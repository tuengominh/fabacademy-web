Cubragon - a modular robot to teach kids how to make (almost) anything

This page is dedicated to describe the process of building my Final Project during the Fab Academy 2020 at Fab Lab Barcelona.

Introduction

I have a constant curiosity for going deep dive into the relationship between human and machine and its subsequent implications. My favorite topic "Life after AI" has led me to the questions of how new jobs will be created and what to do today to nurture the future workforce. Many people would argue intelligent systems and robots will take our jobs, imposing a serious disruption even in the fields related to creativity and emotional intelligence which are supposedly dominated by the human race. In my opinion, they will instead create jobs that have never existed before and our educational system must be revamped to aid younger generations prepare for that. During my first year in Barcelona when I was studying my Master's degree, I was developing an online community-based platform helping postgraduates and life-long learners find courses and educational programs that match the requirements of future technical jobs. I would like to take on a different approach by building a tangible solution that allows people to take ownership of their own learning and personal development.

In addition, I have 2 nephews, one is 6 years old and one is 4 years old. As a dedicated aunt, I've always wanted to build or develop some toys that could engage them in STEAM activities, so when they grow up they will be more than well-prepared to adapt to future jobs. That's why I'm pretty sure that my Final Project should be a modular and connected toy that can teach kids programming - the language of the future, and furthermore, give them the ability to build simple IoT devices.

What will it do?

My idea is to create an off-the-screen and modular robot that can be customized in order for preschoolers to learn basic ideas of programming and robotics. This concept is not new, in fact, it has a long history with many innovative approaches. Even though, the new value I'd like to contribute is how kids can develop a DEBUG mindset. My robot should be able to inform kids (or parents) whether each step is successful or there is an error.

There will be 3 groups of blocks: Input, Logic, Output. Kids will need at least one block of each group to perform the desired task. Who has done this before?

I found some STEAM toys (which are quite successful) with a similar idea, so I think my dragon would be useful and loved by my nephews!

  • Code-a-pillar
  • Cubelets
  • The IFs
  • Matatalab

Our instructors Santi and Xavi also introduced some cool projects by Fab Academy's alumnus that are related to my idea:

  • Self-built IoT devices from LEGO bricks with the amazing idea about connecting blocks using pogo pins
  • DIS/ORDER modular circuit for learning electronics

What makes my idea different?
  • The Logic blocks. Other toys usually simplify the Input - Output relationship with a default If - Then logic. I'd like to invest further in different types of logics in order to go deeper in terms of programming concepts. They could be flow controls (while, for, iteration loop), or operators (and, or, greater than, less than, equal).
  • The Debugging process. After immersing myself into technology for a while, I realized the most important skill kids can develop is a DEBUG mindset. My robot should be able to tell whether there is an error at some point via a LED screen or a bargraph.
  • An Object-Oriented approach. I'm thinking about how to resemble functions with those blocks, maybe a Call output and a Being Called input.

I also think that being hung up on sensors and actuators will both distract me from making an almost-there prototype and distract the kids from trying out as many programming concepts as possible. Hence, my MVP for Fab Academy will have 1 Input block, 1 Output block, and at least 2 Logic blocks.
